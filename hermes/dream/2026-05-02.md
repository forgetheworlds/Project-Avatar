## I. Connection Synthesis — Non-Obvious Links Across Domains

### 1. CineMPC ↔ Avatar Cinematic Shots: The Intrinsic Camera Gap

**What CineMPC does** (arXiv 2401.05272, IEEE TRO 2024): A fully autonomous drone cinematography system that controls both **extrinsic** parameters (drone position, gimbal orientation) AND **intrinsic** camera parameters (focal length, focus distance, aperture) via Model Predictive Control. It can execute a dolly zoom, rack focus between subjects, and maintain depth-of-field composition — all autonomously.

**What Avatar does**: `cinematic_shots.py` with 16 templates controlling only drone position. No gimbal. No camera control. Templates are pre-computed motion curves, not real-time optimized trajectories.

**The connection**: CineMPC's cost function design is modular and separable. Even without intrinsic camera control, Avatar could adopt:
- **Composition cost** (`J_im`): penalizes deviation of subject from rule-of-thirds grid points. This is purely geometric — no camera intrinsics needed. The ESP32-S3 could compute this onboard from bounding box position.
- **Relative pose cost** (`J_p`): penalizes deviation from desired camera-subject distance and angle. This maps directly to Avatar's existing `follow_close` and `orbit_close` templates but with real-time optimization instead of pre-computed curves.
- **Occlusion constraint**: CineMPC models bounding-box overlap as an occlusion cost. Avatar could use this to avoid flying behind trees/buildings that block the subject.

**Actionable insight**: Avatar doesn't need to implement full MPC. A lightweight cost-function evaluator on the MacBook that scores each candidate trajectory against composition rules (subject position, horizon level, motion smoothness) and picks the best would give 80% of CineMPC's benefit at 5% of the compute cost.

**Novel opportunity**: CineMPC is ROS-based and computationally heavy (MPC optimization at each timestep). No one has ported its cinematographic cost functions to a lightweight Python library that runs on a laptop ground station. Avatar could be the first to operationalize CineMPC's theory for hobbyist hardware.

### 2. Game Engine Virtual Cameras ↔ Drone Trajectory Planning

The film industry uses **motion control rigs** (Milo, Bolt, mrmoco.com) for repeatable, precisely-timed camera moves. These rigs plan shots in pre-visualization software (Unreal Engine, Blender) where a virtual camera is animated along a path, then the physical rig executes that path exactly.

**The connection to Avatar**: This is exactly what Avatar's template-based cinematic shots do — but the film industry has decades of R&D on WHAT makes a shot cinematic:

| Film technique | Avatar equivalent | Status |
|---|---|---|
| **Camera shake** (Unreal Engine) | Not implemented | Could add micro-jitter to velocity commands for handheld feel |
| **Focus distance animation** | Not applicable (fixed focus) | Digital zoom in post as substitute |
| **Ease-in/ease-out curves** | S-curve acceleration (DEC-022) | Implemented ✅ |
| **Rule of thirds framing** | Static template parameters | Hardcoded, not adaptive |
| **180-degree rule** | Not implemented | Violated when orbit wraps around subject |
| **Lead room** (space in front of moving subject) | `follow_close` has fixed lead distance | Should scale with subject speed |
| **Headroom** (space above subject's head) | Not implemented | Frame composition is center-weighted |

**Actionable insight**: Avatar's cinematic_shots.py should import cinematography rules from film theory. Each template should have configurable: lead room (as % of frame), headroom (as % of frame), screen direction (maintain left-to-right or right-to-left), and 180-degree line awareness. These are purely geometric — no new hardware needed.

**Cross-domain technique to steal**: Sports AI cameras (Veo, Pixellot, XbotGo) use wide-angle lenses + AI cropping to simulate a camera operator. They capture the entire field at high resolution, then AI tracks the ball/players and crops a 1080p window that follows the action. This is Avatar's "virtual gimbal" — shoot 4K wide, crop a 1080p moving window in post that tracks the subject. The RunCam Thumb Pro at 4K 150° FOV could produce a stabilized, AI-reframed 1080p output that looks like it was shot with a gimbal.

### 3. Greta's 2-Year Failure Log ↔ Avatar's Predictive Maintenance Opportunity

The Greta project (→ [[reference-greta-full-autonomy]]) logged 2 years of failures: 52 props, 7 arms, 6 legs, 2 Pixhawks, 2 Jetsons, multiple ESC/motor sets. Each failure had a precursor: increased vibration (bullet connectors loosening), PWM drift (dying ESC), sudden current spikes (motor bearing seizure).

**The connection**: Avatar's ESP32 path will accumulate telemetry from day one (MPU6050 vibration, per-motor PWM, battery voltage, ToF altitude stability). If Greta's failure patterns are generalizable, Avatar could train a lightweight anomaly detector that flags "this motor's PWM is 15% higher than its pair for level flight — bearing wear imminent."

**No existing project does this.** LiteWing, ESP-Drone, and Crazyflie all log telemetry but none do predictive maintenance. This would be a genuine Avatar differentiator.

**Minimal implementation**: Track 4 metrics:
1. **PWM asymmetry**: max(PWM_i) - min(PWM_i) across motors during hover. If > 10%, one motor is degrading.
2. **Vibration RMS**: MPU6050 accel magnitude variance. If trending up, check props/balance.
3. **Voltage sag slope**: dV/dt during hover. If steepening, battery internal resistance is increasing.
4. **ToF altitude variance**: If increasing at constant throttle, optical flow surface quality is degrading.

A simple threshold-based alert on the MacBook ground station: "Motor 3 may be degrading (PWM +18% vs baseline). Consider replacement before next flight."

### 4. LiteWing's CRTP Protocol ↔ Avatar's MCP Ambitions

LiteWing uses CRTP (Crazy RealTime Protocol) over UDP — a lightweight binary protocol with built-in timeouts, packet types, and port multiplexing. It's battle-tested on Crazyflie and ported to ESP32.

**The connection**: Avatar's architecture uses MCP (JSON-RPC over stdio/HTTP) for agent control, then MAVLink for drone control. That's two translation layers. CRTP is simpler, lower latency, and already implemented on ESP32. 

**What if**: Instead of MAVLink, Avatar's ESP32 spoke CRTP natively, and the MacBook ran a thin CRTP→MCP bridge? This would:
- Eliminate MAVLink parsing on ESP32 (saves flash/RAM)
- Use one protocol end-to-end (CRTP for real-time, MCP for agent)
- Leverage LiteWing's existing CRTP implementation
- Enable Crazyflie client tools for debugging

**Risk**: CRTP is less feature-rich than MAVLink (no missions, no parameters). But for Avatar's use case (velocity/position commands + telemetry), CRTP is sufficient.

### 5. VERTIGO Paper + drone-pathgen → Automated Director's Cut

The VERTIGO paper (arxiv 2604.02467) trains a DPO model to generate cinematic camera trajectories using Unity-rendered previews as a training signal. The drone-pathgen Python library generates Bezier-like smooth paths for drones.

**The connection**: Combine them. Use drone-pathgen to generate candidate trajectories, render them in a lightweight preview (even a 2D top-down view with composition overlay), and score them against cinematographic rules. The highest-scoring trajectory becomes the flight plan. This is essentially "offline CineMPC" — compute the optimal trajectory before takeoff, then fly it open-loop with minor PID corrections.

## III. Novel Research Questions

### RQ1: Can a fixed-camera micro-drone achieve cinematic composition through trajectory alone?

**Context**: CineMPC proves that controlling both camera intrinsics and drone pose is optimal. But Avatar's RunCam Thumb Pro is fixed-focus, fixed-zoom, fixed-mount. The hypothesis: for shots at distances > 5m, trajectory curvature can substitute for gimbal pan, and altitude sweeps can substitute for tilt.

**Experiment design**: Fly 3 orbit shots at 8m radius around a static subject. Shot A: drone faces tangent to orbit (sideways flight — no gimbal needed). Shot B: drone faces subject (requires yaw during orbit — needs gimbal or coordinated yaw+strafe). Shot C: drone faces forward fixed (worst case — subject moves across frame). Compare viewer preference scores. If Shot A scores within 20% of Shot B, the fixed-camera constraint is acceptable for orbits.

**Why it matters**: A gimbal adds 5-15g and $20-50. If trajectory alone is sufficient for 80% of cinematic shots, Avatar saves weight and money.

### RQ2: Can the ESP32-S3 run real-time composition scoring as a reward signal for trajectory optimization?

**Context**: ESP32-S3 can run face detection and color recognition at 10+ FPS. What if it evaluated each frame for composition quality and sent a single scalar score (0-100) to the MacBook instead of full bounding boxes? The MacBook then adjusts trajectory to maximize this score.

**Architecture**:
```
ESP32-S3: frame → face detection → compute composition score
  (rule of thirds position, horizon level, subject size ratio)
  → send score + subject_position to MacBook via UDP (2 floats, 50 bytes)
MacBook: score → gradient ascent on trajectory parameters
  → send velocity adjustments to ESP32 → PID → motors
```

**Why this is novel**: Existing approaches (CineMPC, VERTIGO) do composition optimization on the ground station, which requires full video streaming. Moving the scoring onboard reduces bandwidth by 1000× (50 bytes vs 50KB per frame) and latency by eliminating video encoding/decoding. No published work does this on a $7 microcontroller.

### RQ3: Can Gyroflow's gyro data serve as a real-time vibration quality sensor?

**Context**: Gyroflow uses gyroscope data to compute stabilization transforms in post. But that same gyro data is available in real-time on the ESP32 (MPU6050 at 200+ Hz). Vibration magnitude correlates with: motor degradation, prop imbalance, frame resonance, and wind buffeting.

**Hypothesis**: Real-time vibration RMS can predict video quality before the flight ends. If vibration exceeds a threshold, the drone could autonomously reduce speed, change altitude, or land — trading shot ambition for shot quality.

**Minimal experiment**: Log MPU6050 accelerometer data during hover at various throttle levels. Compute RMS vibration. Compare to Gyroflow's reported "stabilization crop factor" (how much it had to crop to stabilize). If RMS > 0.05g correlates with crop factor > 1.3, then RMS is a valid real-time proxy for post-stabilization quality.

## IV. Architecture Critique

### 🔴 HIGH PRIORITY: The ESP32-as-Sole-FC Architecture Carries Unnecessary Risk

**Current architecture**: ESP32-S3 runs the full flight controller stack — PID loops, sensor fusion, motor mixing, AND UDP communication. All on one microcontroller.

**Problem**: The Deijvoo architecture (ESP32 companion → SBUS → Betaflight FC) and the discovery report's key architectural insight both recommend SEPARATING flight-critical stabilization from experimental code. A dedicated FC (Betaflight, INAV, or even an $15 AIO board) handles the 1kHz stabilization loop. The ESP32 handles the smart stuff (trajectory, UDP, AI). If the ESP32 crashes or the UDP link drops, the FC continues stable flight.

**Greta corroboration**: 6 months lost to PX4 firmware crashes. A dedicated FC running proven firmware would have caught those crashes.

**Recommendation**: Pivot to the Deijvoo architecture:
- **$15 AIO flight controller** (Betaflight, 1S capable, 20×20mm) — handles 1kHz PID, motor mixing, basic sensors
- **ESP32-S3** as companion — handles PMW3901, VL53L1X, GPS fusion, UDP link, AI tasks
- **SBUS/CRSF protocol** between ESP32 and FC — industry standard, 50Hz update rate sufficient for trajectory commands
- **Failsafe**: If ESP32 stops sending SBUS frames, FC enters angle mode + auto-land (built into Betaflight)

**Cost impact**: +$15 for AIO FC, -$0 (ESP32-S3 already in budget). Weight: +3-5g. Risk reduction: substantial.

### 🟡 MEDIUM PRIORITY: Offboard AI Architecture Has a Latency Ceiling

**Current architecture**: MacBook M3 handles all AI — subject detection (YOLO on received video), trajectory planning, LLM mission planning. Commands sent via WiFi UDP.

**Problem**: The MacBook→ESP32→motors pipeline has minimum ~50ms latency (WiFi TX + UDP processing + ESP32 PID loop + motor response). At 4 m/s subject speed, that's 20cm of subject movement per control cycle. The cinema.md document acknowledges this and proposes a lookahead predictor — but this is compensating for architectural delay, not eliminating it.

**What the ESP32-S3 CAN do onboard at low latency (<10ms)**:
- Color blob tracking (simple thresholding in HSV space — a bright orange vest or ball)
- ArUco marker detection (ESP32-S3 can do this at 5-10 FPS)
- Optical flow-based velocity estimation (PMW3901 data → horizontal velocity)
- Simple PID for maintaining distance based on bounding box size

**Recommendation**: Implement a two-tier vision system:
1. **ESP32-S3 onboard (Tier 1, 20Hz, <10ms latency)**: Simple color/blob/ArUco tracking. Handles the fast inner loop of "keep subject at this position in frame." Sends correction velocities directly to PID.
2. **MacBook offboard (Tier 2, 2-5Hz, >100ms latency)**: YOLO person detection, multi-subject tracking, trajectory planning, shot composition scoring. Handles the slow outer loop of "transition to orbit shot, change altitude, identify new subject."

This two-tier approach is inspired by the human visual system: peripheral vision (fast, low-detail, reflexive) vs foveal vision (slow, high-detail, deliberate).

The MacBook becomes the "director" not the "camera operator" — it says "orbit that subject" and the onboard systems figure out how.
